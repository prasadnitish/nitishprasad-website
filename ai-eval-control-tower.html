The problem

AI quality is usually managed with fragments.

Teams often have one spreadsheet for prompts, one notebook for model experiments, another chart for latency, and a separate conversation about release criteria. That makes it hard to treat model performance like an operational system.

What I built
  • N-way LLM-as-judge evaluation — any number of models through a single OpenRouter API key.
  • Domain-weighted rubric: SproutRoute prompts scored on safety (hard floor) and logistical feasibility, seller prompts on accuracy and actionability.
  • A live Model Matrix dashboard that ranks every candidate on quality, latency, cost, and safety side-by-side.
  • Release gate with dataset-specific overrides — a 15-second latency ceiling for trip plans, 3 seconds for seller Q&A.
  • CI enforcement: GitHub Actions runs the smoke suite on every PR and blocks merge on NO-GO.
Why this matters

This is evaluation as product operations, not a data-science exercise. When my production SproutRoute model came back at 7.64 safety — below the 8.0 floor — the tool flagged it before a user would have encountered a wrong car seat law or a closed venue. That is the whole point: turn AI quality into something a PM can manage, discuss, and decide on with evidence.

Scope
  • 7 models benchmarked: Claude Sonnet 4.6 & Haiku 4.5, GPT-5 Mini & Nano, Grok 4 Fast, Gemini 2.5 Flash, DeepSeek-V3.2.
  • Three datasets: 50 seller intelligence, 50 seller support, 25 SproutRoute family-trip prompts with reusable personas.
  • Seven dashboard views including the new N-way Model Matrix.
  • 13 unit tests on judge and gate logic; mobile-responsive UI.

What the gate flagged

Claude Haiku 4.5 — the model currently serving SproutRoute production traffic — scored 7.64 on safety, below the 8.0 hard floor. Grok 4 Fast failed the same check at 7.8. The rest (Sonnet 4.6, GPT-5 Mini, GPT-5 Nano, Gemini 2.5 Flash, DeepSeek-V3.2) all cleared it. The gate exits NO-GO, the CI blocks the release, and a PM has a concrete path: switch to one of the four models that passed and document the tradeoff.

The judge cited real errors in the failing responses: a California car seat law misstatement, Splash Mountain recommended after its 2023 closure, an impossible overnight Paris→Barcelona train routing. These are the kind of low-frequency safety bugs that slip past eyeball QA — which is why the gate is rubric-weighted, not vibes-weighted.

Executive Summary

Good eval tooling doesn’t just score models. It tells a team which one to ship — and why.

The dashboard matters because it keeps quality, safety, cost, latency, and policy gates in the same room. That’s the only way the tradeoffs stay honest enough to drive a launch decision. v2’s biggest move is the safety dimension with a hard floor, which is what turned SproutRoute into a real production signal instead of a demo.

Why it matters

  • I think about AI products after the first launch, not just before it.
  • I know how to turn evaluation into operating logic a team can act on — including my own.
  • I ran the tool against my own shipped product and it found a real regression. That is the acceptance test for any governance system.
