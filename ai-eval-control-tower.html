The problem

AI quality is usually managed with fragments.

Teams often have one spreadsheet for prompts, one notebook for model experiments, another chart for latency, and a separate conversation about release criteria. That makes it hard to treat model performance like an operational system.

What I built
  • LLM-as-judge evaluation on domain-grounded prompts.
  • A/B comparison views across quality, latency, cost, and violations.
  • Drift and release-gate visibility in one interface.
  • A live dashboard that makes the decision logic inspectable, not hidden in a notebook.
Why this matters

This is not just evaluation as a data-science exercise. It is evaluation as product operations. It turns AI quality into something a PM can manage, discuss, and decide on with engineers and leadership.

Scope
  • 24 categories of seller-intelligence prompts.
  • 30-day history and live dashboard experience.
  • Model quality, latency, cost, and violation views.
  • Release gating around explicit thresholds rather than vibes.
Benchmark Story

The comparison is useful because it forces a real decision.

Model A

Claude Sonnet 4.6

Higher quality and low policy violations. More expensive, but comfortably above the release floor.

  • Average quality: 93.0
  • Cost per run: $0.30
  • Policy violations: 0
  • Lowest category score: 83.5
Model B

DeepSeek-V3

Far cheaper, but not safe to wave through blindly. This is exactly the kind of tradeoff the dashboard is supposed to make visible.

  • Average quality: 81.0
  • Cost per run: $0.007
  • Policy violations: 2
  • Lowest category score: 43
Executive Summary

Good eval tooling does not just score models. It helps a team decide what to ship.

The dashboard matters because it keeps quality, cost, latency, drift, and policy gates in the same room. That is the only way the tradeoffs stay honest enough to drive a launch decision.

Why it matters

  • I think about AI products after the first launch, not just before it.
  • I know how to turn model evaluation into operating logic a team can actually use.
  • I am comfortable building PM tools that sit between engineering rigor and product decisions.
