The product

A focused learning app, not a noisy “edtech platform.”

MathQuest helps early learners practice math through short, repeatable sessions with adaptive placement, deterministic hints, and visible progress. The goal was to make it playful without letting it become chaotic.

What I owned
  • Product scope, unit structure, session loop, and content model.
  • Offline-first architecture and the decision to keep all progress local in V1.
  • Parent controls, privacy boundaries, and deterministic tutoring behavior.
  • Theme system, pacing, and the overall feel of the app as a product, not just as a feature list.
Why this matters

The judgment here is restraint: knowing what not to connect, what not to automate, and what not to overcomplicate in the first version.

Scope
  • Portrait-first iPad UX built in SwiftUI.
  • Adaptive placement, review scheduling, narration, and deterministic hints.
  • Parent-gated settings and local diagnostics export.
  • Local Core Data persistence for profiles, attempts, mastery, and history.

Product feel

The calmer choice won over the louder one in almost every design decision: short sessions, one orientation, no ads, no analytics, no surprise states.

Executive Summary

The product is stronger because V1 refuses to be clever in the wrong places.

Kids apps lose trust fast when they lag, break offline, or bury parents under settings and subscriptions. MathQuest avoids that by keeping the core loop local, the logic deterministic, and the parent controls obvious.

Why it matters

  • I can make a smaller product feel complete without stuffing it with extras.
  • I know how to build trust into the product, not just into the copy around it.
  • I can make technical boundaries serve the experience instead of getting in its way.
Deep Dive

The interesting decisions are mostly about boundaries.

Choice 1

Short sessions over endless play

The loop was designed for 5–10 minute sessions because parents need predictable handoff and kids need a clean stopping point.

Choice 2

Portrait lock on purpose

The product stays in portrait to reduce cognitive overhead and keep layout, hit targets, and narration consistent.

Choice 3

Parent trust first

Parent-gated settings, explicit privacy copy, and local progress were treated as core product features, not afterthoughts.

Choice 4

Deterministic hints

Hints are generated rules, not model output. That keeps the tutoring behavior explainable and age-appropriate.

Adaptive placement

Placement starts learners at the right band quickly, which keeps early sessions from feeling too easy or too punishing.

Review scheduling

The composer mixes focus work with review so progress feels earned and repetition stays useful instead of numbing.

Template variety

535 templates is large enough to reduce repetition fatigue but still small enough to maintain intentionally.

Narration and energy

The app uses tone and narration as part of pacing, not just as decoration layered on top of the questions.

Storage

Core Data persistence

Profiles, attempts, mastery, review state, and session history stay on device in V1.

Boundaries

No network in the core loop

The absence of sync and cloud services is a product choice. It keeps the loop fast, dependable, and private.

Diagnostics

Local export instead of remote analytics

Parents and testers can still get useful diagnostics without adding data collection to the product.

UI

One client, one rhythm

The product avoids multi-platform sprawl and uses that saved complexity budget on craft and reliability instead.
