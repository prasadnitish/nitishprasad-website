The problem

The initial ask was too small.

The original request was a dashboard. That would have produced another reporting surface. The better opportunity was an AI seller intelligence product that could drive prioritization, recommendations, and eventually fit directly into Salesforce workflows.

What I owned
  • Product framing and the push from dashboard request to platform direction.
  • Architecture pressure-testing across hybrid inference, RAG, caching, and multi-region compliance.
  • The business case connecting cost structure, latency, adoption, and workflow value.
  • The product path toward API delivery and Salesforce integration.
Why it matters

This is the cleanest example of principal-level product judgment in my portfolio: find the real problem, ignore the too-small framing, make the technical decisions durable, and hold the work to business outcomes that still matter after launch.

Scope
  • Production AI system used by 1,600+ account managers across North America and Europe.
  • Hybrid RAG plus real-time inference architecture built on AWS Bedrock.
  • GDPR-aware multi-region design from the start.
  • A credible path to Salesforce integration instead of a dead-end dashboard.
Executive Summary

The key decision was not “use AI.” It was “choose an operating model that can survive scale.”

Pure real-time inference looked better in a demo but was economically wrong in production. The decision that mattered was combining pre-computation, caching, and selective live inference so the product could stay fast, keep recommendation quality high, and still make sense at scale.

What I would want an interviewer to take away

  • I can reason across business value, architecture, and operating economics.
  • I am comfortable taking ownership when the product definition is still unsettled.
  • I do not stop at the roadmap; I stay close to the decisions that determine whether the system will actually work.
Deep Dive

The technical path is still here. It just comes after the business story.

This is the part I would expect to walk through in a longer interview loop.
Decision 1

Hybrid inference over pure real-time

Pre-compute and cache common seller profiles, then use live inference only where the extra cost is justified. That is what made scale viable.

Decision 2

RAG over fine-tuning

Fresh retrieval-based context was a better fit than repeated retraining on changing seller data.

Decision 3

Multi-region by design

Handled NA and EU residency requirements from the start instead of treating compliance as cleanup work for later.

Decision 4

Platform direction, not dashboard dead end

Built the product toward API delivery and Salesforce integration so it could fit the workflow people already used.

Tradeoff that mattered most

Real-time inference looked better in demos. Hybrid inference was less flashy and more durable. That is the kind of tradeoff I expect principal and staff PMs to make well.

Why this is senior product work

The decision had to balance cost structure, latency, freshness, compliance, scale, and downstream workflow adoption rather than just model quality.

What I would emphasize in an interview

I did not optimize locally for the best model demo. I optimized globally for a product system that could hold up under production constraints.

Customer grounding

The product direction was validated through 15+ customer shadowing sessions, which kept the work anchored in how account managers actually prepared for calls.

Inputs

Seller data and knowledge sources

Operational seller data fed a retrieval layer that grounded recommendations in current account context.

Retrieval

AWS Bedrock knowledge base

Kept answers fresher and easier to operate than a fine-tuned model that would need constant retraining.

Inference path

Batch, cache, then live inference

Common outputs were pre-computed. Only new or edge cases triggered more expensive live calls.

Delivery

Dashboard to API platform

The product was shaped to evolve into Salesforce-integrated recommendations and prioritization rather than staying as a separate reporting layer.

Questions I would expect

Why not pure real-time inference? How did I know the workflow mattered? What did I personally drive versus influence? How did I know the product was worth scaling?

Why I keep leading with this project

It shows the kind of judgment I want to be hired for: problem framing, architecture fluency, and the discipline to keep the product tied to business reality.
